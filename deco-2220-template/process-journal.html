Problem & Context

In the late hours of the night, public transport becomes more scarce and getting home safely becomes more difficult. As people get more intoxicated, the risk of being left stranded and unable to get home increases. Our team aims to design an interface that utilises autonomous cars to deliver intoxicated people to their destination in a safe and efficient manner.

Research Methods

Contextual Analysis

Analysed the current situation using new articles, journals and research papers to understand the scope of potential design solutions. This allowed me to develop a greater understanding on the current technologies to see what has been done. Competitor analysis was further conducted to understand the design patterns in existing products.

Questionnaires

Digitally collect information to gain insight about intoxicated people’s behaviours, attitude and perspective on getting home safely at night. The questions were tailored to analyse how people perceived autonomous vehicles. Furthermore, it explored how intoxicated people currently get home and their outlook on safety

Interviews

Conducted structured and semi-structured interviews to gather insight about existing experiences of users at night and we as the designers to develop empathy towards the people we are designing for. Follow up questions were differed according to individual response, allowing for more meaningful insights.

Research Insight

Analysis through research methods reveals that users are concerned about whether transport is easily accessible after a late night of drinking at the bar and their own personal safety due to alcohol constraining one’s ability to make decision or protect themselves. Online research provides insight on how autonomous cars can be utilised and the consequence of implementing such technology.

Exploration - Idea 1

The first exploration of ideas consisted of a smartphone app, a smartphone widget and a smart watch. The smartphone app allowed users to enter their pick up and drop off location before they drink and essentially schedule an autonomous car for arrival when they are intoxicated. The widget was a simple one button interface on the smartphone that when pressed, would locate the user’s current location for pick up. This idea was scraped due to its simplistic nature as not many screens would have to be designed for a one button interface. For the smart watch, you would set the destination beforehand such as home and once you are ready for pickup, would tap the smartwatch which sends a signal to the autonomous car for pickup. However, all these ideas were fairly similar to one another except that they were on different platforms, so we ended up integrating the features of the widget and smartwatch into the smartphone app to create our first concept.

Figure 1.1 - Early smartwatch concept

Our first concept is the smart phone app designed to keep intoxicated people off the streets and get the home safely by allowing them to call an autonomous car and identifying their location, so they can be picked up. Users can set their pick-up point and destination, time of pick up and will receive a notification on time of arrival so they don’t have to worry about how they will find a way home safely. The app works by having users plan ahead of intoxication so that it will not hinder their ability to make decisions.

Usability testing was used to measure the efficiency, effectiveness and satisfaction of the user when interacting with the app and were able to determine whether the conceptual model of the product matched the user’s mental model. By using a stopwatch, we were able to record the time it took for users to complete certain tasks and with relative ease.Through contextual observation, we observed the user’s facial expressions, actions and posture, revealing a better understanding of the design problems and gathering important feedback for our design. As the user’s progressed through the prototype and spoke their thoughts and expressed their concerns, we were able to find obstacles that disrupt the flow of the interface in specific stages. At the end of the user testing session, we gave the participants a questionnaire making the accuracy and completeness of the information provided reliant of the user’s memory and ability to recall their interactions

The first set of wireframes for the smartphone app were test with 4 young adults. Various usability methods utilised in the user testing, reveal that there were inconsistencies in the app such as some screens missing navigation bars and buttons were different sizes and shapes making the app confusing to navigate. Furthermore, it was noted that there were missing steps in the process to book the autonomous car such as payment process and scheduling a time and date. Some buttons were also very tiny and would be a strain to read for intoxicated people.

For the next set of wireframes for the app, we took the feedback from the previous user testing session into consideration. The buttons for users to progress to the next screen were more prominent so that intoxicated people would know to tap it and the general size of the fonts on the app were increased in size. We also moved a lot unnecessary clutter on the screen, so users would not get confused. We began our second session of user testing on the newly iterated wireframes and it was revealed that the app was a lot easier to navigate due to the layout of elements. However, it was noted that user had no control or freedom due to there being no way to progress to next and previous screens and one user said that they would like the fonts to be larger.

Paper Prototype

Taking the feedback from users on both wireframes, we added features to the app to make it more understandable for intoxicated people. Buttons are now highlighted when the user is prompted to tap it and selections are to be made. A login screen is now added in accommodation with the payment screen, so users can add money to their account and don’t have to worry about payment when under the influence. Notifications now drop down when the autonomous car has arrived for pick up and drop off to inform users. When user testing the first paper prototype, users had trouble progressing to the next screen since there was no confirm button to proceed on two screens. One user had trouble understanding the location service feature, so we had to intervene and explain it to them. On the next iteration, we would like to clarify certain features to have less words and more visuals.

For our last prototype, we uploaded it to interactive prototype app, ‘POP’ and tested it with 3 user who were given the task to call an Autonomous car. Through the addition of extra confirmation screens as well as back buttons and hamburger menus on each screen, users were freely able to navigate the app and book an autonomous car successfully. The design of the app as a whole is very similar to the UBER app so has no real uniqueness that distinguishes it.
